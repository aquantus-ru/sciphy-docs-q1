PHYSICS DICTIONARY

Acceleration:: The rate of change of velocity with respect to time.

Action-Reaction Pair:: Two forces that are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction, acting on two different interacting objects.

Activity:: The number of decays per second of a radioactive sample.

Aliasing:: An effect that causes different signals to become indistinguishable (or aliases of one another) when sampled.

Alpha Particle:: A helium nucleus emitted by some radioactive substances.

Ampere's Law:: Relates the integrated magnetic field around a closed loop to the electric current passing through the loop.

Amplitude:: The maximum extent of a vibration or oscillation, measured from the position of equilibrium.

Angular Momentum:: A vector quantity that represents the product of a body's rotational inertia and rotational velocity.

Antineutrino:: The antiparticle of the neutrino.

Atomic Mass Unit ($u$):: $1/12$ of the mass of a carbon-12 atom ($\approx 1.66 \times 10^{-27}$ kg).

Attenuation:: The reduction in amplitude or intensity of a signal as it travels through a medium.

Bandwidth:: The difference between the upper and lower frequencies in a continuous band of frequencies.

Becquerel ($Bq$):: SI unit of radioactivity (1 decay per second).

Beta Particle:: A high-energy, high-speed electron or positron emitted by the radioactive decay of an atomic nucleus.

Binding Energy:: The energy that would be required to disassemble the nucleus of an atom into its component protons and neutrons.

Biot-Savart Law:: An equation describing the magnetic field generated by a constant electric current.

Bloch Equations:: A set of macroscopic equations that calculate the nuclear magnetization $M$ as a function of time when relaxation times $T_1$ and $T_2$ are present.

Boltzmann Constant ($k_B$):: A physical constant relating the average kinetic energy of particles in a gas with the temperature of the gas.

Boltzmann Distribution:: A probability distribution that gives the probability that a system will be in a certain state as a function of that state's energy and the temperature of the system.

Born Rule:: A postulate stating that probability density is proportional to the square of the magnitude of the wavefunction.

Boson:: A subatomic particle, such as a photon, which has zero or integral spin.

Capacitance:: The ability of a system to store an electric charge ($C = Q/V$).

Coefficient of Friction:: A scalar value ($\mu$) describing the ratio of the force of friction between two bodies and the force pressing them together.

Complex Permittivity:: A measure of how a material affects and is affected by an electric field, including both energy storage and energy dissipation.

Compton Scattering:: The scattering of a photon by a charged particle, usually an electron, resulting in a decrease in energy (increase in wavelength) of the photon.

Conservative Force:: A force with the property that the total work done in moving a particle between two points is independent of the path taken.

Cross-Section ($\sigma$):: A measure of the probability that a specific process will take place in a collision of two particles.

Damping:: The reduction in the amplitude of an oscillation as a result of energy being drained from the system (e.g., friction).

De Broglie Wavelength:: The wavelength associated with a massive particle, inversely proportional to its momentum.

Debye Relaxation:: The dielectric relaxation response of an ideal, non-interacting population of dipoles to an alternating external electric field.

Decay Constant ($\lambda$):: Probability of decay per unit time.

Decibel (dB):: A logarithmic unit used to express the ratio of two values of a physical quantity, often power or intensity.

Dielectric:: An electrical insulator that can be polarized by an applied electric field.

Dimensional Analysis:: A mathematical technique used to check the consistency of equations by comparing the physical dimensions of the terms.

Dipole Moment:: A measure of the separation of positive and negative electrical charges within a system.

Displacement:: A vector quantity representing the straight-line distance and direction from an initial position to a final position.

Dot Product:: An algebraic operation that takes two equal-length sequences of numbers (usually coordinate vectors) and returns a single number.

Dynamics:: The branch of mechanics concerned with the forces that cause or affect motion.

Eigenstate:: A state vector that is unchanged by a transformation (operator), except by a scalar multiplication.

Eigenvalue:: The scalar multiplier associated with an eigenstate.

Elastic Potential Energy:: Potential energy stored as a result of deformation of an elastic object, such as the stretching of a spring.

Elastic Scattering:: Scattering in which the kinetic energy of the incident particles is conserved.

Electric Dipole:: A separation of positive and negative charges.

Electric Flux:: The measure of the flow of the electric field through a given area.

Energy:: A scalar physical quantity that describes the amount of work that can be performed by a force.

Entropy:: A thermodynamic quantity representing the unavailability of a system's thermal energy for conversion into mechanical work, often interpreted as the degree of disorder or randomness in the system.

Equilibrium:: A state in which the net force on an object is zero ($\vec{a} = 0$).

Faraday's Law of Induction:: Predicts how a magnetic field will interact with an electric circuit to produce an electromotive force (EMF).

Fermion:: A subatomic particle, such as a nucleon or electron, which has half-integral spin.

Flip Angle:: The angle to which the net magnetization is rotated relative to the main magnetic field direction.

Force:: An interaction that, when unopposed, will change the motion of an object.

Fourier Transform:: A mathematical transform that decomposes a function (often a function of time, or a signal) into its constituent frequencies.

Frame of Reference:: A coordinate system used to represent and measure the position, orientation, and other properties of objects.

Free Fall:: Motion under the sole influence of gravity.

Free-Body Diagram:: A graphical illustration used to visualize the applied forces and moments on a body.

Frequency ($f$):: The number of occurrences of a repeating event per unit of time.

Gamma Ray:: Penetrating electromagnetic radiation of a kind arising from the radioactive decay of atomic nuclei.

Gravitational Potential Energy:: Energy an object possesses because of its position in a gravitational field.

Gyromagnetic Ratio ($\gamma$):: The ratio of the magnetic moment to the angular momentum of a particle.

Half-Life:: The time taken for the radioactivity of a specified isotope to fall to half its original value.

Hamiltonian:: The operator corresponding to the total energy of the system.

Hamiltonian:: The operator corresponding to the total energy of the system.

Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle:: A limit on the precision with which certain pairs of physical properties, such as position and momentum, can be known.

Inductance:: The property of a conductor by which a change in current in the conductor induces a voltage in both the conductor itself (self-inductance) and in any nearby conductors (mutual inductance).

Inelastic Scattering:: Scattering in which the kinetic energy of the incident particles is not conserved.

Inertia:: The tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion.

Inertial Frame:: A reference frame in which an object stays at rest or moves at a constant velocity unless acted upon by a force.

Instantaneous Velocity:: The velocity of an object at a specific instant in time.

Isotope:: Variants of a particular chemical element which differ in neutron number.

Johnson Noise:: The electronic noise generated by the thermal agitation of the charge carriers (usually the electrons) inside an electrical conductor at equilibrium.

Joule:: The SI unit of work or energy.

Kinematics:: The branch of mechanics concerned with the motion of objects without reference to the forces that cause the motion.

Kinetic Energy:: The energy that it possesses due to its motion.

Larmor Frequency:: The rate of precession of the magnetic moment of a proton around an external magnetic field.

Longitudinal Magnetization ($M_z$):: The component of the magnetization vector parallel to the static magnetic field.

Lorentz Force:: The combination of electric and magnetic force on a point charge due to electromagnetic fields.

Loss Tangent ($\tan \delta$):: The ratio of the imaginary part to the real part of the permittivity ($\epsilon'' / \epsilon'$).

Macrostate:: The state of a system defined by macroscopic properties such as temperature, pressure, and volume.

Magnetic Moment:: A vector quantity that determines the torque a material will experience in an external magnetic field.

Magnitude:: The size or quantity of a physical property, ignoring direction.

Mass Defect:: The difference between the mass of an isotope and its mass number.

Mechanical Energy:: The sum of potential energy and kinetic energy.

Microstate:: A specific detailed microscopic configuration of a system.

Net Force:: The vector sum of all forces acting on an object.

Normal Force:: The support force exerted upon an object that is in contact with another stable object.

Nucleon:: A proton or a neutron.

Nyquist Rate:: The minimum rate at which a signal can be sampled without introducing errors, which is twice the highest frequency present in the signal.

Operator:: A mathematical instruction to perform an operation (like differentiation) on a function.

Orbital:: A mathematical function that describes the wave-like behavior of an electron in an atom.

Partition Function:: A function that describes the statistical properties of a system in thermodynamic equilibrium.

Pauli Exclusion Principle:: A quantum mechanical principle which states that two or more identical fermions cannot occupy the same quantum state simultaneously.

Period ($T$):: The time taken for one complete cycle of vibration.

Permeability ($\mu_0$):: A measure of the ability of a material (or vacuum) to support the formation of a magnetic field within itself.

Permittivity ($\epsilon_0$):: A measure of the resistance of a dielectric substance to the formation of an electric field.

Phase:: The position of a point in time on a waveform cycle.

Photon:: A quantum of electromagnetic radiation.

Polarization:: The density of permanent or induced electric dipole moments in a dielectric material.

Positron:: The antiparticle or the antimatter counterpart of the electron.

Power:: The rate at which work is performed or energy is converted.

Precession:: A change in the orientation of the rotational axis of a rotating body.

Precision:: The degree to which repeated measurements under unchanged conditions show the same results.

Projectile Motion:: A form of motion where an object is thrown near the earth's surface and moves along a curved path under the action of gravity.

Quantization:: The procedure of constraining a physical quantity to discrete values.

Quantization:: The concept that a physical quantity can only have certain discrete values.

Radar Cross Section (RCS):: A measure of how detectable an object is by radar.

Radio Frequency (RF) Pulse:: A burst of electromagnetic wave used to excite the nuclei.

Rayleigh Scattering:: Elastic scattering of light or other electromagnetic radiation by particles much smaller than the wavelength of the radiation.

Relaxation Time:: The time required for a system to return to equilibrium after a disturbance.

Resonance:: The tendency of a system to oscillate with greater amplitude at some frequencies than at others.

Rotating Frame:: A coordinate system that rotates around the z-axis at the Larmor frequency, simplifying the description of magnetic resonance.

Scalar:: A quantity that is fully described by a magnitude alone.

Scalar Component:: The projection of a vector along an axis (e.g., $v_x$).

Shot Noise:: Noise caused by the discrete nature of electric charge.

Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR):: A measure used in science and engineering that compares the level of a desired signal to the level of background noise.

Significant Figures:: Digits in a number that carry meaningful information about its precision.

Simple Harmonic Motion:: Repetitive movement back and forth through an equilibrium, or central, position, so that the maximum displacement on one side of this position equals the maximum displacement on the other side.

Spectral Line:: A dark or bright line in an otherwise uniform and continuous spectrum, resulting from emission or absorption of light in a narrow frequency range.

Spin:: An intrinsic form of angular momentum carried by elementary particles.

Standing Wave:: A wave that remains in a constant position (does not propagate), often formed by interference.

Strong Interaction:: The fundamental force that holds quarks together to form protons and neutrons, and holds the latter together in the atomic nucleus.

Systematic Error:: A consistent, repeatable error associated with faulty equipment or a flawed experiment design.

T1 Relaxation:: The time constant for the recovery of longitudinal magnetization.

T2 Relaxation:: The time constant for the decay of transverse magnetization.

Thermal Equilibrium:: The state in which two substances in physical contact with each other exchange no heat energy.

Trajectory:: The path followed by a projectile flying or an object moving under the action of given forces.

Transverse Magnetization ($M_{xy}$):: The component of the magnetization vector perpendicular to the static magnetic field.

Unit Vector:: A vector of length 1, used to specify a spatial direction (e.g., $\hat{i}, \hat{j}, \hat{k}$).

Valence Electron:: An electron in the outer shell of an atom which can participate in the formation of a chemical bond.

Vector:: A quantity that has both magnitude and direction.

Vector Addition:: The operation of adding two or more vectors together into a vector sum.

Watt:: The SI unit of power.

Wave Number ($k$):: The spatial frequency of a wave ($2\pi/\lambda$).

Wavefunction:: A mathematical description of the quantum state of an isolated quantum system.

Wavelength ($\lambda$):: The distance between successive crests of a wave.

Weight:: The force of gravity on an object ($W = mg$).

Work:: The product of force and displacement.

Zeeman Effect:: The splitting of a spectral line into several components in the presence of a static magnetic field.

Zero-Point Energy:: The lowest possible energy that a quantum mechanical physical system may have.
